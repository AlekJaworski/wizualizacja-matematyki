A geodesic is the path on a manifold that's *as straight as possible*. On a sphere, geodesics are great circles. In flat spacetime, geodesics are straight lines at constant velocity. In curved spacetime, geodesics are the trajectories of free-falling objects.<br><br>"As straight as possible" can be made precise in three equivalent ways: (a) the curve that locally minimizes length (or proper time); (b) the curve along which a tangent vector is parallel-transported (no turning); (c) a curve that satisfies the geodesic equation $\ddot{x}^\lambda + \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\nu} \dot{x}^\mu \dot{x}^\nu = 0$.<br><br>**This is "gravity is geometry" in one statement**: in GR, free-falling objects follow geodesics. There's no force pulling them — they're going as straight as the (curved) spacetime allows. What we *call* gravity in the Newtonian picture is the deviation between the geodesics in curved spacetime and the straight lines we'd expect in flat spacetime.

<!-- example -->
Two friends start at the equator, both walk due north *in parallel*. They converge — meeting at the north pole. No force pushed them together. The geometry of the sphere did. In GR: same story for two test masses dropped near a planet — they converge as they fall, not because of mutual attraction (in a small enough region) but because the spacetime around the planet is curved and their geodesics converge.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Geodesics are always shortest paths"** — locally yes, globally not necessarily. On a sphere, two great-circle arcs connect any two points (the short way and the long way around). Both are geodesics; one is shorter.
- **"Free-falling means accelerating"** — Newton's framing. In GR, free-falling objects have *zero proper acceleration* (no force on them). The "acceleration" you read off coordinates is just the geodesic curving through curved spacetime.
