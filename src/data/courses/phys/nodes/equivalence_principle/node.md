*Locally, you cannot tell the difference between sitting in a uniform gravitational field and accelerating in deep space*. This is the conceptual seed of GR — Einstein 1907, the "happiest thought of my life", arrived at while at the patent office.<br><br>Concretely: an elevator standing on Earth and an elevator accelerating at $g$ in deep space are indistinguishable from inside. Drop a ball — it falls. Stand on a scale — it reads your weight. No experiment performed inside the box can distinguish the two cases.<br><br>This forces a structural choice: gravity isn't a force overlaid on flat spacetime. Gravity is what happens when spacetime is curved, and "free fall" is what trajectories *look like* when you're not pushing them.<br><br>**Local** is critical. Over large enough regions, *tidal* effects show up: in a gravitational field, two test masses separated horizontally fall slightly toward each other (because they fall toward the same center). In an accelerating elevator, they remain parallel. This breaks the equivalence at non-local scales — and this breakdown is what curvature precisely measures.

<!-- example -->
You wake up in a windowless box on a scale. The scale reads your full weight. Are you (a) in your apartment in Warsaw, (b) in a rocket accelerating at $g$ in deep space, (c) on a slowly spinning space station with the right radius? The equivalence principle says: from inside, with no other information, you cannot distinguish (a) from (b). Tidal effects (a 1-meter-tall object's head feels slightly less gravity than its feet) eventually distinguish them, but only if you can measure them.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"It's an exact symmetry"** — only locally. Globally, real gravitational fields and acceleration are distinguishable by tidal effects.
- **"It applies only to mechanical effects"** — the *strong* equivalence principle says it applies to all physical effects, including light propagation and electromagnetic phenomena. This is what predicts gravitational redshift and light bending.
