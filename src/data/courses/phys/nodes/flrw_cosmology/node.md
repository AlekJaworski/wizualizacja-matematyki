The Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric: a solution of the Einstein field equations under two assumptions about the universe at large scales — *homogeneity* (it looks the same at every point) and *isotropy* (it looks the same in every direction).<br><br>$ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a(t)^2 \left[ \frac{dr^2}{1 - kr^2} + r^2 (d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, d\phi^2) \right]$<br><br>Key feature: $a(t)$ is the *scale factor*. Distances between distant galaxies scale as $a(t)$. If $a$ grows with time, the universe expands. The Friedmann equations determine $a(t)$ in terms of the energy content of the universe.<br><br>$k$ is spatial curvature: $k = +1$ closed universe (sphere-like 3-space), $k = 0$ flat, $k = -1$ open (saddle-like). Observation says $k$ is very close to 0.<br><br>This is the framework for all of modern cosmology: Big Bang, expansion history, dark matter, dark energy, the cosmic microwave background — all live in extensions of FLRW.

<!-- example -->
Hubble's law $v = H_0 d$ — distant galaxies recede at speeds proportional to their distance — comes directly from FLRW. The Hubble "constant" $H_0 = \dot{a}/a$ at present time. The galaxies aren't moving *through* space; space itself is expanding, carrying them along. "Raisin bread rising" — every raisin sees every other raisin moving away from it.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Galaxies are moving through space at recession speeds"** — they're (mostly) at rest in their local space; what's happening is that the space *between* them is expanding. This is why galaxies far enough away can recede faster than $c$ without violating SR.
- **"The Big Bang happened at a point"** — happened *everywhere*, simultaneously. The whole universe was hot and dense at $t = 0$; expansion stretched it. There's no special location for the Big Bang.
