Mercury's elliptical orbit doesn't quite close — the perihelion (closest approach to the Sun) advances slightly each orbit. Most of this is explained by Newtonian perturbations from other planets: about 532 arcseconds per century. But there's a stubborn residual: *43 arcseconds per century* unexplained by any Newtonian effect.<br><br>This was known by 1859 (Le Verrier). Various explanations were tried: an unseen planet "Vulcan" inside Mercury's orbit (never found), a non-spherical Sun (too small an effect), modifications to Newton's $1/r^2$ law (ad hoc).<br><br>November 1915: Einstein, in the final stretch of formulating the field equations, computes the precession from his theory. Result: 43 arcseconds per century. *Exactly* the residual. Einstein in a letter to a friend: "for a few days, I was beside myself with joyous excitement."<br><br>This was the *first* successful test of GR — a known unsolved puzzle, sitting in textbooks for 56 years, dispatched on the first try by the new theory. It's the strongest historical argument for GR's correctness.

<!-- example -->
Newtonian-with-perturbations precession of Mercury: ~5557 arcseconds per century. Observed: ~5600 arcseconds per century. Discrepancy: ~43 arcseconds per century. GR contribution from Schwarzschild solution: $\Delta\phi = 6\pi GM/(c^2 a (1-e^2))$ per orbit, evaluating to... 43 arcseconds per century. Match.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"43 arcseconds is small, so it's not important"** — small but exact. The match was the first confirmation that GR was a *quantitatively* correct theory, not just a beautiful idea.
- **"Modern observations might disagree"** — they don't. Lunar laser ranging, radar tracking, and now Mercury orbiter data (MESSENGER, BepiColombo) confirm the GR prediction to ever-tighter precision.
