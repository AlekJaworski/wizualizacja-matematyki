Ripples in spacetime, propagating at the speed of light, generated by accelerating masses. Like sound waves through air or EM waves through the EM field — but the "medium" is the geometry of spacetime itself.<br><br>Linearize the Einstein field equations around flat Minkowski spacetime. The perturbation $h_{\mu\nu}$ on top of $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ obeys (in suitable gauge) a wave equation: $\Box h_{\mu\nu} = 0$ in vacuum. Two polarization states, often called $+$ and $\times$ for their characteristic stretching patterns.<br><br>**Source**: time-varying *quadrupole* moments of mass distribution. Two black holes spiraling in toward merger emit huge amounts of GW energy; spherically symmetric collapse emits zero.<br><br>**History**: Einstein predicted them in 1916. He famously *retracted* in 1936 ("Do gravitational waves exist?" — manuscript rejected by *Physical Review*'s referee, Robertson; Einstein eventually agreed his "no" was wrong). LIGO directly detected them in 2015 (event GW150914): two black holes, ~30 solar masses each, ~1.3 billion light years away, merged in ~0.2 seconds, emitting ~3 solar masses worth of energy as gravitational radiation.

<!-- example -->
GW150914: peak strain at the LIGO detectors was ~$10^{-21}$. That's a length change of $10^{-18}$ m over a 4 km arm — about 1/1000th the diameter of a proton. Detected. Reproducibly. Won the 2017 Nobel Prize.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Gravitational waves are like ripples on a pond"** — the analogy is right qualitatively but the substrate is spacetime geometry, not water. There's no medium to "ripple in"; the geometry itself oscillates.
- **"Any mass that accelerates emits GW"** — only *non-symmetric* acceleration. A spherically pulsating star emits no GW (Birkhoff's theorem). The minimum is a *quadrupole* — needs a non-trivial mass distribution that changes in time.
