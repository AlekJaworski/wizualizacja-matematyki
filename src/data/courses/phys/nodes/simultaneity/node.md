"At the same time" depends on who's asking. Two events that are simultaneous in your reference frame are *not* simultaneous in a frame moving past you. This was Einstein's first conceptual breakthrough on the way to SR.<br><br>The classic thought experiment: a train carriage. Lightning strikes both ends simultaneously *as seen from the platform*. From the platform, the light from each strike reaches the middle observer at the same instant. From inside the (fast-moving) train, the light from the front strike reaches the moving middle observer *first* — so to her, the front strike happened earlier. Same events, two valid descriptions, two different "simultaneities".<br><br>This isn't perception or signaling delay. It's a real, structural feature of spacetime: there is no universal "now". The set of events you call "simultaneous with this moment" is a 3D slice through 4D spacetime, and which slice you pick depends on your motion.

<!-- example -->
A train rushes past a platform at 0.6c. Lightning hits both ends simultaneously in the platform frame. The signal from each strike races toward the observer at the middle of the train. Because she's moving toward the front strike's signal, she meets it earlier. To her, "the lightning at the front of the train happened before the one at the back."

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"It's a perception trick from finite light speed"** — partly: yes, $c$-finiteness is involved. But after correcting for signaling delays, the events are *still* not simultaneous in the moving frame. It's a real feature of how time works at high relative speeds.
- **"There's a 'real' simultaneity, observers just disagree about it"** — no. There is no preferred frame. All inertial frames are equally valid; their disagreements about simultaneity are equally real.
