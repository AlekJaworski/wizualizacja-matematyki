$G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8 \pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}$<br><br>Geometry on the left, matter on the right. Wheeler's slogan: *matter tells spacetime how to curve; spacetime tells matter how to move*.<br><br>$G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} g_{\mu\nu} R$ — the Einstein tensor, built from Ricci and scalar curvature (see [[ricci_and_scalar]]).<br>$T_{\mu\nu}$ — stress-energy tensor, the source.<br><br>One tensor equation, but it unpacks into 10 coupled nonlinear PDEs in the metric components $g_{\mu\nu}$. Solutions are notoriously hard. The exact solutions we have — [[schwarzschild_solution]], [[flrw_cosmology]], Kerr — are precious because they're rare.<br><br>**Where it came from**: November 1915. Einstein vs. Hilbert race in publication. Einstein's published version dated November 25; Hilbert submitted earlier (November 20) but the published version came later, after Hilbert had seen Einstein's correspondence. Historians give priority to Einstein because he had the *physical* test (Mercury's perihelion precession matched), not just the math.

<!-- example -->
Empty space ($T = 0$): $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$, equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$. Schwarzschild solution lives here — vacuum outside a spherical mass. Note: $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ does not mean *no* curvature — Riemann can still be non-zero (Weyl curvature, tidal effects, gravitational waves all live in vacuum). It means no Ricci/no volume contraction of test-particle clouds.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"GR equations are linear"** — wildly nonlinear. Gravity gravitates. Two black holes don't simply add up to a "double field" — the merger is a complex dynamical process.
- **"$\Lambda$ (cosmological constant) is optional"** — observationally, $\Lambda > 0$ (dark energy). The full equation is $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4) T_{\mu\nu}$. Einstein originally added it for static-universe reasons, then called it "his biggest blunder" — but it's back, and observationally required.
