At any event in spacetime, the *future light cone* is the set of events that could be reached by something traveling at $c$ or slower starting from there. The *past light cone* is the set of events that could have caused the event. Outside both: events causally disconnected — they can't affect each other.<br><br>Light cones encode causality. A cause must lie in the past light cone of its effect; otherwise no signal could have traveled fast enough to do the causing.<br><br>In SR, every event has a 45° light cone (with $c = 1$ units) and the cones don't tilt. In GR, the cones tilt as spacetime curves. Near a black hole, light cones tilt toward the singularity until even *outward-pointing* light rays are dragged inward — that's the event horizon (see [[black_holes]]).

<!-- example -->
A supernova at a distance of 1000 light-years explodes today. When can it affect you on Earth? Earliest possible: 1000 years from now, when the light reaches you. Until then it's outside your past light cone — it cannot have any influence on you, no matter what.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Everything in the past affects the future"** — only events in your past light cone do. Events that are simultaneous with you but distant are *causally disconnected*.
- **Confusing light cones with sight cones** — light cone is a structure of *spacetime*; sight cone is what you optically see. They're related but not identical.
