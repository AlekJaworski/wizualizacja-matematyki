Newton's three laws are the foundation of classical mechanics:<br>1. An object at rest stays at rest, an object in motion stays in motion, *unless* acted on by a force. This is a *definition* of "force" as much as a law about motion: force is what changes velocity.<br>2. $\vec{F} = m \vec{a}$. Force equals mass times acceleration. Given the forces, integrate twice to get the trajectory.<br>3. Every action has an equal and opposite reaction. Forces come in pairs.<br><br>This is the framework GR will replace for gravity. But every other corner of physics — friction, contact forces, electromagnetism — still works in the Newtonian style. GR is specifically the gravity rewrite.

<!-- example -->
You push a 5 kg block with 10 N of force, no friction. Acceleration: $a = F/m = 10/5 = 2$ m/s². After 3 seconds, velocity is $2 \times 3 = 6$ m/s. After 3 seconds, distance traveled is $\frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 3^2 = 9$ m. Newton's laws predict the entire trajectory from forces and initial conditions.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"$F = ma$ defines force"** — partly. It also tells you how mass and acceleration relate *given* a force. The deeper definition of force comes from specific force laws (gravity, Coulomb, etc.).
- **"Inertial frames are special because Newton's laws hold in them"** — they're the frames where Newton's laws hold *without correction terms*. In an accelerating frame, you have to add fictitious forces (centrifugal, Coriolis) to make $F = ma$ work.
