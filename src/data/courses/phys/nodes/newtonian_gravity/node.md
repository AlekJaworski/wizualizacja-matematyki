$F = G m_1 m_2 / r^2$. Every two masses attract with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance. $G \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11}$ N·m²/kg² is the gravitational constant.<br><br>This single rule explains apples and planets. Newton's *Principia* (1687) was the first physics theory that unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics.<br><br>**It's also wrong in three identifiable ways**, and those three failures are the historical motivation for GR:<br>1. *Action at a distance.* If the Sun vanished, when does Earth's orbit change? Newton: instantly. Reality (we now know): 8 minutes — gravity propagates at $c$.<br>2. *Mercury's perihelion.* Newton predicts the precession of Mercury's orbit slightly off from observation — by 43 arcseconds per century. The discrepancy was known by 1859 and unexplained for 56 years.<br>3. *Light bending.* Treating photons as having effective mass, Newton predicts some bending near the Sun, but only half what's observed.<br><br>GR fixes all three exactly.

<!-- example -->
Earth's mass: $5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg. Distance from Earth's center to surface: $6.37 \times 10^6$ m. Gravitational acceleration at surface: $g = G M_E / R_E^2 = 9.81$ m/s². The famous number is just one calculation from $F = G m_1 m_2 / r^2$ with $m_1 = M_E$, $r = R_E$.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Gravity propagates instantly in Newton"** — yes, but that was a *bug*, not a feature. Newton himself was uneasy about it.
- **"Mass is what creates gravity"** — in Newton, yes. In GR, *mass-energy* (including pressure and momentum flux) creates gravity. See [[stress_energy]].
