Force is *not* "what causes motion". This is the deepest re-education the physics curriculum demands. Free fall feels like nothing — astronauts on the ISS are *falling* the entire time, and they float. The thing that feels like a force is the ground pushing up at you.<br><br>Stand on a bathroom scale. The scale reads your weight. But what is the scale actually measuring? Not gravity directly — it's measuring the *contact force* the floor exerts on you, equal in magnitude to gravity (because you're not accelerating). Cut the floor away — you're in free fall — and the scale (now falling with you) reads zero. *Gravity didn't change.* The contact force vanished, and that's the whole reading.<br><br>This is the seed of the [[equivalence_principle]]: locally, free fall and "no gravity" are indistinguishable. An astronaut in orbit and an astronaut in deep space (with engines off) feel identical from inside the cabin.<br><br>Once you accept this, gravity stops being a "force" in the Newtonian sense and starts looking like *what happens by default* when you're not being pushed by anything else. The trajectory of a thrown ball isn't fighting gravity — it's the natural path through curved spacetime. Everything in [[geodesic]] follows from this.

<!-- example -->
You're in an elevator. The cable snaps. For the few seconds before impact, you and everything in the elevator float — your dropped phone hangs in the air next to you. From inside, you cannot tell whether you're falling or floating in deep space. The bathroom scale reads zero. *Gravity hasn't gone anywhere*. What's gone is the floor pushing up at you.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Astronauts on the ISS are weightless because there's no gravity at that altitude"** — wrong. Gravity at 400 km altitude is about 89% of surface gravity. They feel weightless because they're falling — alongside the station — and free fall feels like nothing.
- **"A thrown ball is constantly fighting gravity"** — no. The thrown ball is in free fall the entire time; nothing is fighting anything. The trajectory is what objects in free fall do.
- **"Force = mass × acceleration, so if there's acceleration there must be a force"** — true in Newtonian mechanics with a fixed background. In GR, *acceleration* of a free-falling object is zero (it's following a geodesic); what we *call* gravitational acceleration in Newton is an artifact of choosing the wrong frame.
