The speed of light is the same for every observer, regardless of their motion. Walk toward a flashlight at 0.5c and you'd expect to measure the light coming at you at 1.5c — but you don't. You measure $c$. Walk away at 0.5c — still $c$.<br><br>This isn't philosophy. It's the result of the Michelson–Morley experiment (1887), designed to detect Earth's motion through a hypothetical "ether" by measuring tiny differences in the speed of light along vs. across the direction of Earth's orbital motion. The result was null. Confused everyone for 18 years.<br><br>Einstein 1905: stop trying to explain it. Treat it as a *postulate*. Everything weird in special relativity — time dilation, length contraction, $E = mc^2$ — falls out of accepting that one experimental fact and reasoning carefully about its consequences.

<!-- example -->
Imagine a flashlight on a train moving at 0.6c past you on a platform. The train's headlight beam: from the train's perspective, light leaves the bulb at $c$. From your platform perspective, the light *also* leaves the bulb at $c$ — not at $c + 0.6c = 1.6c$ as Galilean addition of velocities would predict. Both observers measure the same $c$. The Galilean addition rule breaks at high speeds.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"It's a definition that c is constant"** — no. It's an *experimental fact* that came as a surprise to physicists in the late 1800s.
- **"At low speeds, light addition obeys Galilean rules"** — at low speeds the corrections are tiny and Galilean addition is *almost* right. But $c$-invariance holds exactly, at all speeds.
