Moving clocks tick slow — by a factor $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$ relative to a clock at rest in the observer's frame.<br><br>The cleanest derivation uses a *light clock*: two parallel mirrors with a photon bouncing between them. In the clock's rest frame, the photon travels straight up and down — short path. In a frame where the clock is moving sideways, the photon traces a zigzag — longer path. But the photon's speed is $c$ either way (see [[c_invariance]]). Longer path at the same speed = more time per tick. The moving clock ticks slower as observed from outside.<br><br>This isn't an illusion or a mechanical artifact. *All* physical processes — clocks, biological aging, atomic decay — slow down at high relative velocity. Confirmed experimentally by particle decays in accelerators (muons live longer when moving fast), atomic clocks on flying aircraft (Hafele–Keating 1971), and GPS satellites (where SR time dilation reduces to ~7 μs/day correction).

<!-- example -->
A particle moving at 0.99c has $\gamma \approx 7.1$. A clock comoving with it ticks 7.1× slower as seen from a stationary frame. If the particle's "internal clock" measures 1 second, the lab clock has measured 7.1 seconds.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"It's a perception effect"** — no. The slow-down is real and persistent. Two clocks separated, moved at high speed, and brought back together will *actually* show different elapsed times.
- **"Both observers see the other's clock as slow — there's no fact of the matter"** — both *do* see the other's clock as slow (this is symmetric for inertial frames). The fact of the matter only emerges when one of them accelerates (the twin paradox). Then the asymmetry is real, and the traveling twin returns younger.
