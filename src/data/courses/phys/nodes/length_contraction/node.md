Moving rulers are short, by the same factor $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$ as time dilation. A meter stick moving past you at 0.99c looks 14 cm long.<br><br>This is not a perspective effect or a measurement-process artifact — it's a real change in the *spatial extent* of moving objects, in the rest frame of the observer measuring it. It's the spatial analog of [[time_dilation]]: time stretches, space contracts, both by the same $\gamma$.<br><br>Length contraction is what makes high-energy cosmic-ray muons reach the ground despite a half-life that would otherwise be too short — from their frame, the atmosphere is contracted, the trip is short, they live long enough.

<!-- example -->
A meter stick rushes past you at 0.6c. To you, it's $1 \times \sqrt{1 - 0.36} = 0.8$ m long. To someone moving with the stick, it's still 1 m. Both descriptions are correct — length is frame-dependent.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **Contracting in all directions** — only along the direction of motion. Perpendicular dimensions are unaffected.
- **"The stick gets compressed"** — no internal stress. Contraction is geometric, not mechanical.
