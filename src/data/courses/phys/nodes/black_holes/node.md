A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is strong enough that not even light can escape. The boundary — the event horizon — is at the Schwarzschild radius $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ for a non-rotating, uncharged black hole.<br><br>Inside the horizon, the radial coordinate $r$ becomes *timelike* — meaning the singularity at $r = 0$ is in your future, not at a place. You cannot avoid it any more than you can avoid tomorrow.<br><br>**Kinds**:<br>- **Schwarzschild**: non-rotating, uncharged. Simplest case. (See [[schwarzschild_solution]].)<br>- **Kerr** (1963): rotating. More physically realistic — most astrophysical black holes have angular momentum. The rotating black hole drags spacetime around with it (frame dragging).<br>- **Reissner-Nordström**: charged. Mostly mathematical interest.<br>- **Kerr-Newman**: rotating and charged.<br><br>**Observations**: stellar-mass black holes detected via X-ray binaries (matter falling in heats up, emits X-rays). Supermassive black holes ($10^6$–$10^{10}$ solar masses) at galactic centers — Sagittarius A* in our galaxy. Event Horizon Telescope produced the first direct image of a black hole's "shadow" in 2019 (M87) and Sgr A* in 2022.

<!-- example -->
Falling into a black hole: from outside, an observer watching you fall in sees you *slow down* and *redshift* as you approach the horizon — never quite reaching it (asymptotically). From your perspective, you cross the horizon in finite proper time, with nothing locally dramatic at the crossing — the horizon is an empty region of vacuum spacetime, just one with the special property that it's a one-way membrane. After crossing, tidal forces eventually pull you apart and you hit the singularity.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Black holes suck things in"** — they don't have any extra "pulling power" beyond their mass. If the Sun were replaced with a 1-solar-mass black hole today, the planets' orbits would be unchanged. Black holes only become deadly when you get close enough to fall through the horizon.
- **"Nothing escapes a black hole"** — in classical GR, true. Quantum effects (Hawking radiation) cause black holes to slowly evaporate. For stellar-mass holes, the evaporation timescale far exceeds the age of the universe.
