GPS works because every smartphone trusts the precise timestamp on signals from satellites in medium Earth orbit. The system depends on time synchronization to nanoseconds — and at nanosecond precision, both special and general relativity are not just relevant but *required* for the system to function.<br><br>Two effects compete:<br>- **Special relativistic time dilation** (motion-based): satellites moving at ~3.9 km/s tick *slower* than ground clocks by ~7 μs/day.<br>- **Gravitational time dilation** (GR): satellites at ~20,000 km altitude experience weaker gravity than ground clocks, so they tick *faster* by ~45 μs/day.<br><br>Net: ~38 μs/day faster on the satellites. Without correction, GPS positions would drift by ~10 km/day — useless within minutes.<br><br>The system pre-corrects the satellite clocks at launch, ticking them slightly slow on the ground so they run "right" once in orbit. Every smartphone, every car GPS, every fitness watch is using GR every second. **Most concrete daily-life win for the theory.**

<!-- example -->
Without relativistic corrections, a GPS fix would drift error at ~10 km/day. After 30 minutes of operation, your phone would put you ~200 m off — far enough to be on the wrong side of a building. With corrections, accuracy is ~5 m.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"GPS just uses Newtonian physics"** — Newtonian alone is wrong by 38 μs/day. GR is essential, not optional.
- **"GR is purely theoretical with no everyday application"** — false. You used it on the way to work this morning.
