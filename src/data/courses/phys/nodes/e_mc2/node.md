Mass and energy are forms of the same conserved quantity. A particle at rest has energy $E_0 = mc^2$. The full relation, including motion, is $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$ — energy squared = momentum-energy squared plus rest-energy squared.<br><br>Consequence: even a *stationary* object has a vast amount of intrinsic energy, $mc^2$ per unit mass. For 1 kg: $9 \times 10^{16}$ J — the energy of about 20 megatons of TNT. Nuclear reactions (and stellar fusion) tap a fraction of this.<br><br>$E = mc^2$ also says: putting energy *into* something *increases its mass*. A hot brick weighs slightly more than a cold one. A compressed spring weighs more than a relaxed one. Mass is energy in a particular form.<br><br>This matters for GR because the source of gravity isn't just rest mass — it's *all* energy and momentum, packaged into the [[stress_energy]] tensor.

<!-- example -->
Hydrogen fusion in the Sun: 4 protons → 1 helium-4 nucleus + 2 positrons + 2 neutrinos. Mass of 4 protons: 4 × 1.0073 u = 4.0292 u. Mass of helium-4: 4.0026 u. Difference: 0.0266 u, converted to energy via $E = mc^2$: about 26.7 MeV per fusion event. That's where sunlight comes from.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"$E = mc^2$ only applies to nuclear reactions"** — applies to everything. It's a statement about *all* energy.
- **"Mass increases with speed"** — older textbook framing ("relativistic mass"). Modern usage: mass is invariant; *energy* increases with speed. The notion of variable mass causes confusion; avoid it.
