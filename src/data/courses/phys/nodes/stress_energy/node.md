The source of gravity in GR is not just mass. It's *all* forms of energy and momentum, packaged into a 4×4 symmetric tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$.<br><br>Components:<br>- $T_{00}$: energy density (including rest-mass energy via $E = mc^2$).<br>- $T_{0i}, T_{i0}$: momentum density / energy flux.<br>- $T_{ij}$: stress (pressure on the diagonal, shear off-diagonal).<br><br>**Pressure gravitates.** Energy gravitates. Even electromagnetic field energy curves spacetime. This is qualitatively different from Newton, where mass alone is the source.<br><br>For a perfect fluid: $T_{\mu\nu} = (\rho + p/c^2) u_\mu u_\nu + p g_{\mu\nu}$ — depends on energy density $\rho$, pressure $p$, and the fluid's 4-velocity $u^\mu$.<br><br>The stress-energy tensor is conserved: $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ (covariant divergence vanishes). This conservation drove Einstein to the *correct* form of the field equations — earlier versions failed conservation and he had to reject them.

<!-- example -->
A box of pressurized gas. Newton: gravitates only by its rest mass. GR: also gravitates by its kinetic energy, by its pressure, by anything contributing to the stress-energy. For ordinary matter, the rest-mass term dominates; for radiation-dominated universes (early cosmology), pressure is comparable to energy density and matters significantly.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"$T$ is just mass-density spread out"** — it's a 4×4 tensor with 10 independent components, not a single scalar. Different physics (matter, radiation, vacuum) gives different tensor structures.
- **"GR sources are the same as Newton's"** — Newton: mass density. GR: stress-energy tensor. These are equal only in the non-relativistic limit (low energy density, low velocity, low pressure relative to energy density).
