GR predicts that mass bends light. Specifically, a light ray grazing the Sun should be deflected by $\Delta\phi = 4GM/(c^2 b)$, where $b$ is the impact parameter — about 1.75 arcseconds for the Sun.<br><br>Newtonian gravity, treating photons as if they had effective mass, predicts only *half* this much (0.87 arcseconds). GR gets the factor 2 right because of how spacetime geometry, not just gravitational acceleration, interacts with light's null geodesic.<br><br>**Eddington 1919**: led an expedition to Príncipe (off the coast of West Africa) and Sobral (Brazil) to observe the May 29 total solar eclipse. The eclipse blacks out the Sun, allowing photographs of stars near the Sun. Comparing positions to night-sky reference plates: stars near the Sun's disk appeared shifted by exactly the GR prediction.<br><br>The result was announced November 6, 1919 at a joint Royal Society / Royal Astronomical Society meeting. Within days, *The Times* of London ran "Lights all askew in the heavens" on its front page. Einstein became globally famous overnight.

<!-- example -->
A star directly behind the Sun would have its light bent around the Sun, appearing displaced from its true position. GR predicts a 1.75 arcsecond shift for a star whose light just grazes the solar limb. Eddington's measurements: $1.61 \pm 0.30$ arcseconds and $1.98 \pm 0.16$ arcseconds at his two stations. Both consistent with GR within errors, both inconsistent with the Newtonian half-prediction.

<!-- mistakes -->
- **"Light bending was the first GR test"** — Mercury's perihelion was first chronologically (Einstein computed it in November 1915 while finalizing the field equations). Light bending was the first *new* prediction (verified in 1919, three years after the theory).
- **"Eddington's data was conclusive"** — the error bars were big. Subsequent eclipses and modern radio interferometry have reduced uncertainties to <1%; GR continues to nail the prediction.
